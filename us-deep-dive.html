The U.S. is an extreme outlier. Among 14 high-income countries analyzed, America's maternal mortality rate is more than double most peers — and 22× higher than Norway, which recorded zero maternal deaths.
The Racial Gap Is Staggering
Black women face 3.5× the maternal mortality risk of white women — a disparity that persisted even as overall rates fell in 2023.

Age Is a Major Risk Factor
Women over 40 face nearly 5× the mortality risk of women under 25.
At 59.8 deaths per 100K, women 40 and older face nearly 5× the risk of those under 25 (12.5). Rising maternal age in the U.S. contributes to overall mortality trends.

A "maternity care desert" is a county with no hospital offering obstetric care, no birth center, and no obstetric providers. Women in these areas must travel long distances for prenatal care and delivery.

Why Is the U.S. an Outlier?
Structural factors that distinguish America from its peers:
No Universal Healthcare

~8 million women of reproductive age are uninsured. The U.S. is the only high-income country without universal coverage.

Severe Midwife Shortage

Only 4 midwives per 1,000 births vs. 58 in Norway. Midwifery care is associated with better outcomes but is underutilized.

No Paid Parental Leave

The only wealthy nation with no federally mandated paid maternity leave. Peer countries guarantee 14+ weeks.

Limited Postpartum Care

2 in 5 women skip their postpartum checkup. Other countries guarantee home visits within a week of birth.
